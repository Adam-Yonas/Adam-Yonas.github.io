DeSCENT Deployer

Space Systems Design Studio

DeSCENT (Demonstration of Suborbital ChipSats Ejected from New Shepard Test Flight) is a ChipSat deployment mission developed through Cornell’s Space Systems Design Studio in collaboration with Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory. The project centers on a two-bay deployer designed to house and eject 100 ChipSats from the JANUS platform aboard Blue Origin New Shepard for suborbital atmospheric sensing and reentry testing.

Overview

The DeSCENT deployer was designed to package, retain, and eject 100 ChipSats at apogee from the JHU APL JANUS platform. The system uses a machined aluminum chassis with 3D-printed inserts, spring-loaded launch bars, fishing-line retention, and a burn-wire circuit to execute a staged deployment sequence: first opening the doors, then ejecting the ChipSats.

  • 100 ChipSat deployment at ~100 km apogee
  • Two internal bays with 50 ChipSats each
  • Staged burn-wire triggered deployment sequence
  • Spring-driven ejection targeting ~4 m/s velocity
  • Integrated with JHU APL JANUS payload platform

Details

Design, integration, and validation.

Architecture

The deployer uses a machined 6061-aluminum primary structure with 3D-printed internal racks to organize and constrain ChipSat motion. The system is split into two bays, each containing 50 ChipSats, with spring-loaded launch bars providing the ejection impulse.

  • Machined aluminum baseplate, side walls, and door structure
  • PETG-CF printed racks enabling rapid iteration
  • Two-bay architecture with independent deployment rows
  • Geometry constrained to maintain ChipSat electrical interface contact
Deployment Mechanism

Deployment is executed through a staged burn-wire actuation system. Torsion-spring doors release first, followed by compression-driven launch bars that eject the ChipSats. Fishing line provides lightweight retention and is cut using nichrome burn wires triggered by a 30 V signal.

  • Two-stage release: doors → launch bars
  • Fishing-line (Dyneema) retention system
  • Nichrome burn-wire actuation
  • Sequenced deployment for controlled release
Mechanical Design Decisions

Mechanical performance was driven by friction reduction, packaging constraints, and repeatability. Sliding interfaces were optimized using Delrin contact surfaces and controlled finishes to ensure consistent deployment behavior across both bays.

  • Delrin sliders reduced friction against aluminum interfaces
  • Surface finish control improved sliding consistency
  • 3D-printed racks enabled rapid geometry iteration
  • Routing redesign eliminated fishing-line entanglement
Testing & Results

The deployer was validated through repeated full-system deployment tests using representative test chips and a 30 V actuation signal. Testing identified failure modes in friction, routing, and tolerancing, directly informing iterative design improvements.

  • Repeated full deployment tests with loaded test chips
  • Video analysis used to diagnose inconsistencies
  • Iterative refinement of sliders, routing, and tolerances
  • Validated staged deployment sequence
Requirements & Performance

The deployer was designed to satisfy JANUS and Blue Origin constraints while remaining well below payload mass limits. The mechanical system delivers controlled spring-driven energy to ensure safe and repeatable ChipSat separation.

  • 100 ChipSat capacity
  • ~4 m/s ejection velocity
  • ~19.8 J spring energy per row
  • ~4.48 kg development unit mass
  • Designed for vacuum and launch-load environments
Outcome

The project progressed from concept to a fully functional engineering development unit capable of repeatable staged deployment. Mechanical refinements enabled reliable release behavior and established a foundation for flight-ready system development.

  • Built and validated full deployer system
  • Demonstrated consistent staged deployment
  • Enabled transition toward flight-integrated hardware
