DeSCENT Deployer

Space Systems Design Studio

DeSCENT (Demonstration of Suborbital ChipSats Ejected from New Shepard Test Flight) is a ChipSat deployment mission developed through Cornell’s Space Systems Design Studio in collaboration with Johns Hopkins University Applied Physics Laboratory. The project centers on a two-bay deployer designed to house and eject 100 ChipSats from the JANUS platform aboard Blue Origin New Shepard for suborbital atmospheric sensing and reentry testing.

Overview

The DeSCENT deployer was designed to package, retain, and eject 100 ChipSats at apogee from the JHU APL JANUS platform. The system uses a machined aluminum chassis with 3D-printed inserts, spring-loaded launch bars, fishing-line retention, and a burn-wire circuit to execute a two-stage deployment sequence: first opening the doors, then ejecting the ChipSats.

  • Designed to deploy 100 ChipSats at 100 km apogee
  • Two internal bays holding 50 ChipSats each
  • Burn-wire triggered door release and ChipSat ejection
  • Integrated with the JHU APL JANUS payload platform
  • Targeted exit velocity of 2–4 m/s

Details

Design, integration, and validation.

Architecture

The deployer uses a machined aluminum primary structure with 3D-printed internal racks to organize and constrain ChipSat motion. The system is split into two bays, each loaded with 50 ChipSats, with top-mounted doors for retention and spring-loaded launch bars for ejection.

  • Machined aluminum baseplate, end walls, side walls, and doors
  • 3D-printed side and middle racks for ChipSat guidance
  • Two launch bars driven by internal compression springs
  • Designed around manufacturability and iterative refinement
Deployment Mechanism

Deployment relies on fishing-line retention loops and a two-stage burn-wire actuation sequence. One burn event releases the doors, and a second releases the launch bars to eject the ChipSats. This architecture allowed independent control of the two major deployment steps while keeping the mechanism compact and mechanically simple.

  • Fishing-line retention for doors and launch bars
  • Nichrome burn-wire activation through a dedicated circuit
  • Independent triggering of door and bar release
  • 30 V high-signal interface requirement at apogee
Mechanical Design Decisions

Several design changes were driven by testing. Internal racks were transitioned to 3D-printed parts to reduce manufacturing complexity and improve adaptability to future ChipSat geometry changes. Delrin sliders were used at the launch-bar interfaces to reduce friction against the aluminum end walls, and the launch bar and stopper geometry were iteratively resized to improve consistency.

  • 3D-printed racks replaced more difficult machined-slot concepts
  • Delrin sliders reduced sliding friction in the end-wall guides
  • Launch stopper height was adjusted to improve packing reliability
  • Peg-based routing reduced fishing-line entanglement during deployment
Testing & Results

The deployer was repeatedly tested using a dedicated stand, substitute acrylic test chips, and a 30 V power supply to simulate the flight deployment signal. Testing was used not only to verify operation, but also to identify failure modes and drive redesigns in tolerancing, routing, and hardware interfaces.

  • Repeated full deployment tests with loaded test chips
  • Video review used to diagnose deployment inconsistencies
  • Launch bars, sliders, and routing refined through iteration
  • Engineering development unit achieved full functional deployment tests
Requirements & Performance

The system was designed to satisfy JANUS and Blue Origin interface constraints while remaining well below the overall payload mass limit. The development unit mass was approximately 4.48 kg (about 10 lb), far below the 50 lb integrated mass requirement, and the closed deployer geometry remained within the required payload envelope.

  • 100 ChipSat capacity
  • 2–4 m/s target ejection velocity
  • 30 V deployment signal requirement
  • ~4.48 kg deployer mass for the development unit
  • Designed for near-vacuum and low-temperature deployment conditions
Outcome

The project advanced the DeSCENT mission from legacy concepts to a fully functioning engineering development unit capable of repeated full deployment demonstrations. The work established the deployer architecture, validated the core release sequence, and generated the mechanical refinements needed to move toward a flight-ready unit.

  • Built and validated a functional engineering development unit
  • Demonstrated controlled door and ChipSat release
  • Created a stronger path toward JANUS integration and future flight hardware
