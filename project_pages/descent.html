Overview

The DeSCENT deployer was designed to package, retain, and eject 100 ChipSats at apogee from the JHU APL JANUS platform. The system uses a machined aluminum chassis with 3D-printed inserts, spring-loaded launch bars, fishing-line retention, and a burn-wire circuit to execute a staged deployment sequence: first opening the doors, then ejecting the ChipSats.

  • Designed to deploy 100 ChipSats at ~100 km apogee
  • Two internal bays holding 50 ChipSats each
  • Staged burn-wire triggered door and launch-bar release
  • Spring-driven ejection targeting ~4 m/s exit velocity
  • Integrated with the JHU APL JANUS payload platform

Architecture

The deployer uses a machined 6061-aluminum primary structure with 3D-printed internal racks to organize and constrain ChipSat motion. The system is split into two bays, each containing 50 ChipSats, with spring-loaded launch bars providing the ejection impulse. The design balances structural rigidity, packaging density, and manufacturability for iterative development.

  • Machined aluminum baseplate, side walls, and door structure
  • PETG-CF printed racks enabling rapid iteration and geometry updates
  • Two-bay architecture with independent ChipSat rows
  • Geometry constrained to maintain ChipSat power-switch engagement
Deployment Mechanism

Deployment is executed through a staged, burn-wire actuated release system. Torsion-spring doors are released first, followed by compression-driven launch bars that eject the ChipSats. High-strength fishing line restrains both subsystems and is cut via nichrome burn wires triggered by a 30 V signal from the JANUS platform.

  • Two-stage release: door opening followed by launch-bar ejection
  • Fishing-line retention using Dyneema for high strength and low mass
  • Nichrome burn-wire actuation for reliable release in vacuum conditions
  • Sequenced release architecture for controlled deployment
Mechanical Design Decisions

Mechanical performance was driven by friction reduction, packaging constraints, and repeatability. Sliding interfaces between launch bars and structure were optimized using Delrin contact surfaces and controlled surface finishes to ensure consistent motion and ejection behavior.

  • Delrin sliders reduced friction against aluminum end-wall interfaces
  • Surface finishes controlled to improve sliding consistency
  • 3D-printed racks enabled rapid geometry iteration and tolerance refinement
  • Fishing-line routing redesigned to prevent entanglement during release
Testing & Results

The deployer was validated through repeated full-system deployment tests using representative test chips and a 30 V actuation signal. Testing identified failure modes in friction, routing, and tolerancing, directly informing iterative design improvements.

  • Repeated full deployment tests with loaded surrogate ChipSats
  • Video analysis used to diagnose inconsistent release behavior
  • Iterative refinement of launch bars, sliders, and retention routing
  • Validated staged release sequence under realistic conditions
Requirements & Performance

The deployer was designed to satisfy JANUS and Blue Origin interface constraints while remaining well below payload mass limits. The mechanical system delivers a controlled spring-driven ejection impulse to ensure safe separation from the vehicle at apogee.

  • 100 ChipSat capacity across two deployment bays
  • ~4 m/s ejection velocity driven by spring-loaded launch bars
  • ~19.8 J stored spring energy per deployment row
  • ~4.48 kg development unit mass (well below system limits)
  • Designed for vacuum, thermal, and launch-load environments
Spring-Loaded Deployment System

Preloaded compression springs store ~19.8 J of energy per row and drive the launch bars to achieve ~4 m/s ChipSat ejection. The system was tuned for consistent release and minimal friction across both bays.
