Alpha CubeSat

Space Systems Design Studio

Alpha CubeSat is a 1U spacecraft developed through Cornell’s Space Systems Design Studio (SSDS) as a flight-ready platform for a free-flying light sail technology demonstration. The mission combined low-cost spacecraft manufacturing, in-house integration, and rigorous environmental validation to build a student-assembled CubeSat around real deployment and launch constraints.

Overview

Alpha CubeSat served as a flight hardware program focused on building, assembling, and validating a 1U spacecraft within CubeSat deployer constraints. My work centered on manufacturing deployer-interface hardware, completing physical fit checks, and supporting assembly and environmental test activities needed to move the vehicle toward flight readiness.

  • 1U CubeSat platform with deployer-constrained external interfaces
  • Rail hardware manufactured for flight, EDU, and spare configurations
  • Fit-checked against NanoRacks CubeSat Deployer requirements
  • Integrated and verified through physical assembly and environmental test support
My Role: Manufacturing Lead (Aug 2021 - May 2024)
  • Precision machining and fabrication of CubeSat rail hardware
  • Dimensional verification and deployer interface fit checks
  • Physical assembly and mechanical integration support
  • Environmental testing support during validation activities
Details

Manufacturing, integration, and validation.

Manufacturing & Fabrication

My primary contribution focused on manufacturing the rail interfaces required for CubeSat deployment. This included producing multiple rail sets for flight, engineering development, and spare use, while translating CAD geometry into real machining operations with tight dimensional control.

  • Machined multiple rail sets for flight, EDU, and spare hardware
  • Performed stock prep, dimensioning, slot milling, drilling, and tapping
  • Used 1/4 in and 1/8 in tooling to achieve final slot and edge geometry
  • Prepared hardware for anodization and post-process revalidation
Precision & Tolerancing

A major challenge was maintaining deployer-compatible geometry while working across metric CAD dimensions and imperial machine tooling. Rail fabrication required iterative dimension control, corner-rounding refinement, and machining decisions that accounted for both shop tolerances and downstream anodization growth.

  • Maintained CubeSat outer dimensions below the 100.6 mm fit requirement
  • Developed a repeatable 1 mm corner-rounding process from cutter geometry
  • Worked within ~0.1 mm machine tolerance limits
  • Accounted for anodization thickness in final fit-check planning
Assembly & Integration

Beyond fabrication, I supported physical assembly and interface validation of the CubeSat hardware. Rail fit checks were completed with helicoils, switch interfaces, and the 3D-printed actuation attachment installed to confirm that the assembled hardware met deployer and switch-actuation needs.

  • Integrated rails onto the CubeSat for mechanical fit verification
  • Validated switch actuation through the installed rail and printed interface hardware
  • Supported mechanical assembly of flight-relevant hardware configurations
  • Used CAD-based fit-check tools to verify compatibility with the NanoRacks deployer envelope
Environmental & Physical Validation

I supported the hardware validation flow used to confirm Alpha’s readiness for flight-style conditions. This included fit-check verification before and after hardware finishing, as well as support of broader assembly and environmental test activities used to catch faults early and preserve access to hardware throughout integration.

  • Repeated dimensional fit checks before final hardware use
  • Supported vibration and environmental test readiness activities
  • Helped verify hardware accessibility and assembly quality during integration
  • Contributed to a multi-stage validation approach for flight hardware
Key Hardware Requirements

The deployer-interface hardware had to satisfy strict CubeSat envelope and actuation constraints while remaining manufacturable with student-lab resources. Rail geometry, slot sizing, and tapped features all had to support reliable deployer integration without exceeding external dimensional limits.

  • CubeSat external envelope kept below 100.6 mm
  • All three interface switches required reliable actuation
  • Rail features sized for deployer compatibility and repeatable fastening
  • Hardware validated across flight and EDU hardware sets
Outcome

My work helped move Alpha from machined components to integrated, deployer-compatible hardware ready for assembly and validation. By focusing on rail manufacturing, fit verification, and physical testing support, I contributed to the mechanical readiness of a student-built CubeSat progressing toward flight.

  • Delivered deployer-interface rail hardware for flight and test configurations
  • Verified mechanical compliance against deployer fit constraints
  • Supported assembly and environmental validation of flight hardware
