Alpha CubeSat

Space Systems Design Studio

The Space Systems Design Studio (SSDS) at Cornell, founded by Professor Mason Peck, is a student-led spacecraft development group focused on rapid, hands-on mission design and real flight hardware. The team operates with an emphasis on building, testing, and launching small satellites and novel space systems, giving students direct experience with end-to-end spacecraft development. Through projects ranging from CubeSats to deployable systems and distributed ChipSat architectures, SSDS advances low-cost, scalable approaches to space exploration while maintaining a strong focus on flight validation, integration, and operating within real mission constraints such as ISS deployment and rideshare launches.

Overview

Alpha CubeSat is a 1U spacecraft developed through Cornell’s Space Systems Design Studio (SSDS), a student-led program founded by Professor Mason Peck focused on rapid, flight-driven spacecraft development. The project serves as a platform for designing, building, and validating real flight hardware, with an emphasis on deployable systems and end-to-end spacecraft integration. Within SSDS, Alpha CubeSat contributes to a broader effort to develop low-cost, flight-ready technologies that can be tested in real space environments, including ISS deployment.

Details

Design, analysis, and outcomes.

Design

The spacecraft was designed as a compact 1U platform capable of housing both standard CubeSat subsystems and a deployable payload. Mechanical design focused on tight packaging constraints, structural integrity under launch loads, and reliable deployment interfaces. The system integrates a light sail payload that is stowed within the CubeSat volume and released using a controlled mechanism, with multiple deployer architectures explored to improve reliability and performance.

Analysis

Design decisions were driven by structural and environmental requirements associated with launch and on-orbit operation. This included evaluating load paths, ensuring compliance with CubeSat form factor constraints, and verifying that deployment mechanisms would function reliably after exposure to vibration and thermal cycling. Trade studies were performed on deployment strategies, including door configurations and actuation methods, to balance simplicity, reliability, and repeatability.

Manufacturing

Hardware was fabricated and assembled with a focus on repeatability and integration with existing CubeSat standards. This included machining structural components, assembling deployer mechanisms, and integrating subsystems within the constrained 1U volume. Iterative prototyping was used to refine deployment systems and ensure compatibility between mechanical, electrical, and structural elements.

Testing / Results

The system was validated through environmental testing aligned with spaceflight requirements, including vibration and thermal vacuum testing. Deployment mechanisms were tested extensively to ensure consistent release and operation under simulated flight conditions. As part of SSDS’s flight-oriented approach, Alpha CubeSat contributes to ongoing efforts to demonstrate reliable deployable systems in space, particularly in support of solar sail missions.
