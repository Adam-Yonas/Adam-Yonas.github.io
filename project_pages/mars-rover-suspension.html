Cornell Mars Rover Suspension

Cornell Mars Rover

The Cornell Mars Rover project is a student-led effort focused on designing and building a competition rover capable of traversing challenging terrain and performing mission-relevant tasks. The system is developed for the University Rover Challenge (URC), with an emphasis on mobility, durability, and reliable subsystem integration. The project provides hands-on experience in full-system engineering, from mechanical design to field testing in realistic off-road environments.

Overview

The Drives and Suspension subteam of the Cornell Mars Rover project focuses on designing the rover’s mobility system to ensure reliable traversal over rough, uneven terrain. This includes developing the drivetrain, wheel interfaces, and suspension architecture to maximize stability, traction, and load distribution. My work centered on adapting the JPL-inspired rocker-bogie suspension for our rover while also developing an alternative bevel gear-based drivetrain configuration. This involved reworking the suspension geometry for our specific constraints, integrating drivetrain components within the rocker-bogie system, and designing a bevel gear solution to improve torque transmission and packaging within the wheel assemblies. The goal was to create a robust, modular mobility system that could handle real terrain conditions while remaining manufacturable and serviceable for rapid iteration and competition use.

Details

Mobility design, structural analysis, and fabrication.

Design

The rover is built around a rocker-bogie suspension system to enable stable traversal over uneven terrain while maintaining wheel contact and load distribution. Mechanical design focused on optimizing the chassis, suspension geometry, and drivetrain to balance strength, weight, and mobility. Components were designed for modularity and serviceability, allowing rapid iteration and repair during testing and competition.

Analysis

Design decisions were supported by structural and kinematic analysis to ensure the rover could withstand terrain-induced loads and operate effectively under varying conditions. This included evaluating suspension performance, load distribution across wheels, and drivetrain torque requirements. Trade studies were conducted to optimize wheel design, gear ratios, and material selection for durability and efficiency.

Manufacturing

The rover was fabricated using a combination of machined components and off-the-shelf hardware, with a focus on manufacturability and rapid iteration. Assembly required integration of structural components, drivetrain elements, and suspension systems, with attention to alignment and tolerance control. Iterative builds were used to refine performance and address issues identified during testing.
