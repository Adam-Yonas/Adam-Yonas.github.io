Sailing to the Stars

Space Systems Design Studio

The Space Systems Design Studio (SSDS) at Cornell, founded by Professor Mason Peck, is a student-led spacecraft development group focused on rapid, hands-on mission design and real flight hardware. The team operates with an emphasis on building, testing, and launching small satellites and novel space systems, giving students direct experience with end-to-end spacecraft development. Through projects ranging from CubeSats to deployable systems and distributed ChipSat architectures, SSDS advances low-cost, scalable approaches to space exploration while maintaining a strong focus on flight validation, integration, and operating within real mission constraints such as ISS deployment and rideshare launches.

Overview

Sailing to the Stars is a solar sail development effort within Cornell’s Space Systems Design Studio (SSDS), focused on designing, packaging, and deploying compact light sail systems from CubeSat platforms. The project aims to demonstrate low-cost, propellant-free propulsion by leveraging solar radiation pressure, while advancing deployable structures that can be reliably stowed within highly constrained volumes and deployed on orbit.

Details

Design, integration, and results.

Design

The system is centered around a deployable light sail integrated within a CubeSat form factor. The sail is packaged using a compact folding architecture and deployed using a controlled release mechanism, with design iterations exploring single-door and double-door configurations to improve deployment reliability. Structural design focuses on maintaining stiffness and integrity during deployment while minimizing mass, and incorporates materials such as thin-film membranes and deployable booms or frames to achieve the required surface area.

Analysis

Design trade studies were conducted to evaluate deployment strategies, packaging efficiency, and reliability under spaceflight conditions. This included assessing hinge and door mechanisms, retention systems, and actuation methods to ensure consistent deployment. Consideration was also given to the effects of solar radiation pressure on the deployed sail, as well as structural behavior during and after deployment to ensure stability and avoid entanglement or incomplete deployment.

Testing / Results

The system was tested through repeated deployment trials to validate reliability and consistency of the sail release mechanism. Testing focused on ensuring complete and clean deployment, with particular attention to failure modes such as snagging, incomplete extension, or asymmetrical deployment. Results from these tests informed design refinements and contributed to ongoing SSDS efforts to mature solar sail technologies for future flight demonstrations.
