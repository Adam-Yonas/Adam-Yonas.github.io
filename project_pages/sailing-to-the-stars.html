Sailing to the Stars

Space Systems Design Studio

Sailing to the Stars is an ISS-based light sail deployment experiment developed through Cornell’s Space Systems Design Studio (SSDS). The mission was built to evaluate free-flying light sail deployment in microgravity using a modular architecture with a reusable avionics cube and interchangeable payload sections, enabling repeated deployment testing across multiple hardware variants.

Overview

The project compares two deployer architectures, single-door and double-door payload sections, across six planned ISS deployment tests. On-orbit video and IMU data are used to study post-deployment sail and deployer stability, with the goal of informing future light sail spacecraft and deployment system designs.

  • ISS microgravity light sail deployment experiment
  • Six deployments using two deployer architectures
  • Reusable avionics cube with swappable payload sections
  • Video and IMU data collected for stability analysis
  • Designed to guide future light sail mission hardware

Details

Design, integration, and validation.

Architecture & Design Trade

The mechanical system was built around a modular separation between a reusable avionics cube and disposable payload sections. Two payload architectures were developed to evaluate deployment behavior: a single-door design derived from prior Cornell sail deployment work, and a double-door concept intended to reduce tip-off by balancing door opening forces.

  • Single-door: spring-latched release with fishing-line retention
  • Double-door: opposed door opening to reduce induced angular momentum
Mechanical System

Each payload section integrated the deployment hardware needed for controlled sail release, including filament actuators, limit switches, safety pins, and battery pack interfaces. The folded sail was packaged within a compact CubeSat-scale structure and released by cutting a restrained fishing line.

  • Filament actuator-triggered release mechanism
  • Mechanical and electrical safety pin interface
  • Limit switch integration for safe operation
  • Compact sail folding and deployer packaging
Avionics Integration

The reusable avionics section housed the PCB and hard-drive reaction wheel inside a common outer shell. Alignment features and keyed interfaces ensured repeatable mating between the avionics cube and each payload section, while also preventing connector misalignment during assembly.

  • Reusable avionics module shared across deployment tests
  • Integrated PCB holder and electrical connector alignment
  • Reaction wheel packaged within the avionics shell
  • Mechanical interface designed for repeatable payload swapping
Testing & Results

The deployers were validated through repeated mechanical and full deployment tests with integrated avionics, battery packs, and folded sails. Testing focused on actuator reliability, safety pin power cutoff, door opening performance, and clean sail release, with results feeding directly into flight readiness for ISS operations.

  • Repeated full-system deployment tests
  • Validation of filament actuator cut reliability
  • Assessment of deployment symmetry and failure modes
  • Flight-like hardware assembly and integration checks
Deployment

Delivered flight-ready units, verified at NASA facilities in Florida. Launched July 2025 on Crew-11, with deployment tests conducted aboard the ISS.
